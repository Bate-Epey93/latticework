How to actually internalize these

Most people collect mental models the way they collect bookmarks: acquired, admired, never used. Internalization is a different operation from reading. It has a known mechanism, and this manual is built around it.

The core problem: a model you can recognize is not a model you can use. Recognition ("ah yes, inversion, I've read about that") lives in a different memory system than recall-under-pressure ("I'm stuck on this decision — let me invert it"). The whole game is moving models from the first system to the second. Four mechanisms do this reliably; everything in this manual is one of them wearing different clothes.

Mechanism 01

Active recall

Retrieving a model from memory strengthens it far more than re-reading it. Testing is not assessment — testing is the studying. Every drill card in this manual asks you to produce the model before showing it. Struggle is the signal that learning is happening; ease is the signal that it isn't.

Mechanism 02

Spaced repetition

Memory decays on a curve, and each successful recall flattens it. Reviewing right before you'd forget is maximally efficient. The Drill tab runs a Leitner system: five boxes with intervals of 0, 1, 3, 7, and 21 days. Grade yourself honestly and the schedule handles itself.

Mechanism 03

Elaboration (Feynman)

Explain one model per day, out loud or in writing, to an imagined intelligent 12-year-old — no jargon allowed. Where you reach for a technical term, you've found the exact edge of your understanding. Rewrite that spot in plain words. This is also the direct training for your communication goal: the curse of knowledge dies here.

Mechanism 04

Application & connection

A model becomes permanent when it has hooks into your real life. Once a week, take one live decision or problem and run it through two or three models deliberately (the Apply tab gives you scripts). And after learning any model, ask: which other models does this one touch? The Lattice tab exists to make those threads visible.

Why connections matter more than the models themselves. Isolated facts decay; networked facts reinforce each other. Every link between two models is a second retrieval path — if you forget the name, you can rebuild the idea from its neighbors. This is what Munger meant by a latticework: the lattice is the knowledge. A model with five connections is roughly five times harder to lose than a model with none.

Memory hooks: dual coding

Every model in this manual carries a hook — a single vivid image or phrase. This is dual coding: an idea stored both verbally and visually has two addresses in memory. As a poet you have an unfair advantage here. Don't skip the hooks; if one of mine doesn't land, write your own. A hook you authored beats a hook you borrowed.

The six-week protocol

Roughly 20 minutes a day. Reading week by week prevents overload; drilling daily across all learned models creates interleaving — mixing domains is harder and feels worse, and that difficulty is precisely what builds the discrimination skill of knowing which model a situation calls for.

WEEK 1
Core + Life (10 models). Read all ten. Drill daily. Feynman one per day. Weekend: journal one real situation through first principles or inversion.
WEEK 2
Decision-Making (8 models). These are the highest-leverage set. Run one real decision through the 5-Minute Triage in the Apply tab. Keep drilling weeks 1–2 together.
WEEK 3
Build / Problem-Solving (5 models). Apply five-whys or hypothesis-driven debugging to one live problem (a workflow bug, a stuck draft, a skill file). Drill everything so far.
WEEK 4
Psychology (5 models). Fieldwork week: catch one fundamental attribution error and one incentive explanation in the wild. Note them. Drill continues.
WEEK 5
Marketing + Communication (10 models). Rewrite one real piece of your writing using the pyramid principle and curse-of-knowledge checks. Drill continues.
WEEK 6
Consolidation. No new material. Spend sessions in the Lattice: pick a node, trace every thread, explain each connection aloud. Then blank-page test: reproduce the whole lattice from memory on paper. The gaps you find are your review list.

After week six, the Leitner schedule naturally thins to a few cards a day. Maintenance is: keep drilling what comes due, keep the weekly one-decision-through-the-models habit, and once a month redraw the lattice from memory. That's the whole system. It is boring and it works — which is itself a lesson about systems over intentions.

The field protocols

The 5-minute decision triage

For any non-trivial decision
  1. Door test. Is this reversible (two-way door)? If yes: decide fast with ~70% of the information you wish you had, and move. Speed is the correct answer to reversible decisions.
  2. If irreversible: slow down deliberately. Write the decision as a sentence. Check you're solving the real problem (five whys on the decision itself).
  3. Sketch expected value. List 2–4 plausible outcomes, rough probabilities, rough payoffs — including non-monetary ones. You're not computing; you're exposing which assumption the decision actually hinges on.
  4. Invert. "What would make this a disaster?" Write three answers. Can you cheaply block any of them? That's your margin of safety.
  5. Second order. "And then what?" — once for the best outcome, once for the worst.
  6. Emotional authority check. Your own system: if the decision is irreversible and the emotional wave hasn't completed, the correct move is to wait. Clarity is downstream of the wave, not upstream.

Expected value worksheet

When outcomes are uncertain and comparable
  1. List each plausible outcome of each option.
  2. Assign a probability to each (they must sum to ~100% per option). Use base rates as your starting point, not your feelings.
  3. Assign a value to each outcome — money, hours, energy, relationship cost. Convert to one rough common unit.
  4. Multiply and sum: EV = Σ (probability × value).
  5. Then override consciously if needed: EV assumes you can play many times. For one-shot, ruin-risk decisions (health, reputation, total savings), a positive EV with a catastrophic branch is still a no. Never risk what you can't afford to lose for something you don't need.

Bayes in 60 seconds (odds form)

When new evidence arrives and you must update a belief
  1. State your prior odds before the evidence. ("I thought it was 3-to-1 that the client would renew.")
  2. Ask of the new evidence: how much more likely is this observation if my belief is true than if it's false? That ratio is the likelihood ratio. (Slow email replies: maybe 2× more likely if they're leaving.)
  3. Multiply: posterior odds = prior odds × likelihood ratio. (3:1 × 1:2 = 3:2 — still leaning renew, but weaker.)
  4. The discipline is the habit, not the arithmetic: evidence shifts belief in proportion to how surprising it would be otherwise. Weak evidence moves you slightly. You are never at 0% or 100%.

Pre-mortem script

Before committing to any plan or project
  1. Set the scene: "It is twelve months from now. This project failed completely."
  2. Write the story of why — past tense, specific, no hedging. Generate at least five distinct causes.
  3. Sort causes into: preventable now / detectable early / pure risk.
  4. For each preventable cause, add one concrete safeguard to the plan today. For each detectable one, define the tripwire metric that would tell you it's happening.

This works because imagining the failure as already real bypasses optimism bias — prospective hindsight surfaces risks that "what could go wrong?" never does. It is inversion, weaponized.

10 / 10 / 10 + regret minimization

When emotion and stakes are both high
  1. How will I feel about this choice in 10 minutes? (Captures the immediate emotional pull.)
  2. In 10 months? (Captures medium-term consequences — the ones second-order thinking cares about.)
  3. In 10 years? (Captures identity and values — at 80, looking back, which choice minimizes regret?)
  4. When the three horizons disagree, the long horizon usually deserves the tiebreak — but notice why the short horizon is screaming. It's data about a cost you should mitigate, not ignore.

The weekly decision journal

Sunday, 15 minutes — the habit that compounds everything
  1. Pick the week's one most significant decision or problem.
  2. Write: what I decided, what I expect to happen, probability I'm right, and which models I used.
  3. Once a month, reread old entries and score the predictions. This is your calibration training — the only reliable way to find out whether your confidence and your accuracy actually match.
  4. When you catch a recurring miss (always underestimating time, always over-trusting enthusiasm), that pattern is worth more than any model in this manual. Write it on the inside cover.
